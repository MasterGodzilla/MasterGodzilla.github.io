**Author: GPT-5.5 Pro**

A recent Zhihu article, "Your DeepSeek mHC May Not Need the 'm'," reported an interesting experimental observation: in from-scratch training experiments on Qwen3 1.7B and 8B dense models, setting the mHC residual mixer to the identity---i.e., letting $H^{res}=I$---outperformed mHC, mHC-lite, and even orthogonal mHC [1].

This observation deserves attention. The engineering implementation of mHC is not particularly elegant: it requires dynamically generating a residual mixing matrix, then approximately projecting it onto the Birkhoff polytope via Sinkhorn-Knopp normalization. mHC-lite also points out that a finite number of Sinkhorn-Knopp iterations does not guarantee exact double stochasticity, and efficient implementations often require additional CUDA kernels or specialized optimizations [3][4].

So the natural question is:

**Why does iHC work? What expressive power does it lose compared to mHC?**

This article attempts to provide an explanation at the level of expressive power.

The conclusion is:

**Full-rank mHC is essentially just a stream-basis gauge of iHC.**

In other words, as long as the mHC residual map does not drop rank, it does not bring any new end-to-end expressive power.

However:

**Rank-deficient mHC enables a reset-and-reuse that pure iHC cannot exactly accomplish.**

So, pure iHC, while clean, is indeed missing one thing: forget / reset.

The resulting compromise is gated iHC. More precisely, norm-gated iHC:

$$
Y_{l+1}
=
D(g_l)Y_l
+
H^{post}_lF_l(H^{pre}_lY_l)
$$

where $D(g_l)$ is a diagonal gate with scale normalization.

It preserves the simplicity of identity HC, avoids full $n\times n$ residual mixers and Sinkhorn-Knopp normalization, while recovering the part of mHC that cannot be absorbed by a full-rank gauge: allowing certain stream directions to be reset and then reused by subsequent writes.


# 1. Preliminaries: What Are mHC and iHC?

A standard Transformer residual connection can be written as:

$$
x_{l+1}
=
x_l
+
F_l(x_l)
$$

The idea behind Hyper-Connections is to expand a single residual stream into multiple residual streams [2].

Let the residual state at layer $l$ be $X_l\in\mathbb R^{n\times d}$, where $n$ is the number of streams and $d$ is the hidden dimension of each stream.

A layer of mHC can be abstractly written as:

$$
X_{l+1}
=
A_lX_l
+
r_lF_l(p_lX_l)
$$

where $A_l=H^{res}_l$ is the residual stream mixer, $p_l=H^{pre}_l$ is responsible for reading one input from multiple streams to feed into the transformer block, and $r_l=(H^{post}_l)^T$ is responsible for writing the transformer block's output back into multiple streams.

The core constraint of mHC is that $A_l$ belongs to the Birkhoff polytope, meaning:

$$
A_l\mathbf 1=\mathbf 1
$$

$$
\mathbf 1^TA_l=\mathbf 1^T
$$

$$
(A_l)_{ij}\ge 0
$$

That is, a doubly stochastic matrix that is nonnegative and has row and column sums equal to 1 [3].

The motivation for mHC is to restore the identity mapping / norm-preserving property in the residual connection, avoiding the training instability caused by free residual mixing in the original HC [3].

In practice, however, it usually obtains the doubly stochastic matrix through Sinkhorn-Knopp normalization. The main criticism of mHC-lite is precisely this: finite Sinkhorn-Knopp iterations have an approximation gap, and efficient implementation has additional engineering overhead [4].

iHC is the simplest version: fixing $A_l=I$. Then:

$$
X_{l+1}
=
X_l
+
r_lF_l(p_lX_l)
$$

That is, the residual streams themselves no longer mix across streams; each stream maintains its own identity path.

Note that iHC does not mean "no cross-stream communication."

Because $p_l$ and $r_l$ can still be dynamically generated from all flattened streams. That is, cross-stream information fusion can still occur within $H^{pre}_l$ and $H^{post}_l$.

An important intuition from the Zhihu article [1] is also this: $H^{res}=I$ only cancels the explicit stream mixing on the residual path, but does not cancel dynamic read/write.


# 2. Theorem 1: Full-Rank mHC Is Equivalent to iHC

Let's start with the most important positive result.

## Theorem

Assume that the mHC residual map at each layer is static and full-rank, i.e., $\det(A_l)\ne 0$. Then for any such mHC network, there exists an iHC network of the same depth and width such that the two are end-to-end equivalent.

More precisely, the residual mixing of full-rank mHC is just a change of stream basis. iHC can fix the residual path as identity, and then absorb this change into new read/write maps and the output head.

## Proof

The mHC update is:

$$
X_{l+1}
=
A_lX_l
+
r_lF_l(p_lX_l)
$$

Define the cumulative stream basis:

$$
P_0=I
$$

$$
P_{l+1}=A_lP_l
$$

Because each $A_l$ is full-rank, each $P_l$ is invertible.

Now let the hidden state of iHC, $Y_l$, represent the same mHC state $X_l$ in another basis:

$$
X_l=P_lY_l
$$

Construct iHC:

$$
Y_{l+1}
=
Y_l
+
\tilde r_lF_l(\tilde p_lY_l)
$$

Let $\tilde p_l=p_lP_l$, and let $\tilde r_l=P_{l+1}^{-1}r_l$.

Then, the input seen by the iHC block is:

$$
\tilde p_lY_l
=
p_lP_lY_l
=
p_lX_l
$$

So it is exactly the same vector fed into $F_l$ as in mHC:

$$
F_l(\tilde p_lY_l)
=
F_l(p_lX_l)
$$

Now look at the state update:

$$
Y_{l+1}
=
Y_l
+
P_{l+1}^{-1}r_lF_l(p_lX_l)
$$

Since $X_l=P_lY_l$, we have $Y_l=P_l^{-1}X_l$. Therefore:

$$
Y_{l+1}
=
P_l^{-1}X_l
+
P_{l+1}^{-1}r_lF_l(p_lX_l)
$$

And because $P_{l+1}=A_lP_l$, we have:

$$
P_l^{-1}
=
P_{l+1}^{-1}A_l
$$

Substituting, we get:

$$
Y_{l+1}
=
P_{l+1}^{-1}A_lX_l
+
P_{l+1}^{-1}r_lF_l(p_lX_l)
$$

That is:

$$
Y_{l+1}
=
P_{l+1}^{-1}
\left(
A_lX_l
+
r_lF_l(p_lX_l)
\right)
$$

So:

$$
Y_{l+1}
=
P_{l+1}^{-1}X_{l+1}
$$

Equivalently:

$$
X_{l+1}
=
P_{l+1}Y_{l+1}
$$

By induction, $X_l=P_lY_l$ holds for every layer.

If the final output is a linear readout $o=CX_L$, then iHC can use $\tilde C=CP_L$ to obtain:

$$
\tilde CY_L
=
CP_LY_L
=
CX_L
$$

QED.

## Explanation

This theorem shows that full-rank mHC does not truly exceed iHC.

Full-rank residual mixing performs a change of basis, not new computation.

It rotates the stream coordinate system. But as long as this coordinate transformation is invertible, iHC can use a different set of read/write parameters to write out the same computation.

This also explains why simple permutations cannot serve as counterexamples.

If $A_l$ is just a permutation matrix, it is even more obvious: it merely renumbers the streams. The embedding, read, write, and output head can all absorb this rearrangement.

So the part of mHC that may truly exceed iHC is not full-rank mixing.

It is rank collapse.


# 3. What mHC Actually Adds: Reset and Reuse

A full-rank matrix changes basis.

A rank-deficient matrix discards information.

If mHC allows $\text{rank}(A_l)&lt;n$, it can do something that pure iHC finds difficult:

**Erase an old stream direction, then reuse it.**

This is not a gauge.

This is a reset.

The doubly stochastic matrix cumulative collapse mentioned in the Zhihu article [1] can also be understood from this perspective: if residual mixing gradually compresses into low-rank directions over many layers, then it indeed brings about some form of forgetting / homogenization. But whether this forgetting is what we want, and whether it requires a full Sinkhorn matrix to achieve, is another question.

Below is a small counterexample showing that the reset-and-reuse capability of rank-deficient mHC is indeed not something that pure iHC can exactly absorb.


# 4. Counterexample: Rank-Deficient mHC Can, Pure iHC Cannot

To make the counterexample closer to the transformer FFN, instead of a simple squaring function, we use a scalar no-bias SwiGLU primitive:

$$
\phi(t)
=
t\cdot\text{swish}(t)
=
t^2\sigma(t)
$$

SwiGLU belongs to the GLU variants; Shazeer's GLU variants paper discusses using GLU / GEGLU / SwiGLU forms in Transformer FFNs and reports improvements over ReLU / GELU [6].

This counterexample is not saying that a wide MLP in a real large model cannot approximate some function. It shows that, under the same depth, the same residual-stream mechanism, and the same scalar no-bias SwiGLU primitive, a rank-deficient residual mixer provides an exact operation that a pure identity residual path does not have.

Let $n=2$, $d=1$.

Define $e=(1,1)^T$, $q=(1,-1)^T$, and the rank-1 averaging matrix:

$$
J=\frac12ee^T
$$

It is doubly stochastic, but not full-rank:

$$
\text{rank}(J)=1
$$

and:

$$
Jq=0
$$

That is, it kills the zero-sum direction.

Let the input be $x=(x_1,x_2)^T$, and define the differential coordinate $Q=q^Tx=x_1-x_2$.

## mHC Construction

Layer 1:

$$
X_1
=
Jx
+
q\phi(q^Tx)
$$

That is, first erase the raw difference direction with $J$, then write a new feature into the $q$ direction.

Layer 2:

$$
X_2
=
X_1
+
\frac12e
\phi
\left(
\frac12q^TX_1
\right)
$$

Now compute what the second layer reads:

$$
\frac12q^TX_1
=
\frac12q^TJx
+
\frac12q^Tq\phi(Q)
$$

Because $q^TJx=0$ and $q^Tq=2$, we have:

$$
\frac12q^TX_1
=
\phi(Q)
$$

Finally, using stream-sum readout:

$$
o_{mHC}(x)
=
e^TX_2
$$

we get:

$$
o_{mHC}(x)
=
e^Tx
+
\phi(\phi(q^Tx))
$$

That is:

$$
o_{mHC}(x)
=
x_1+x_2+\phi(\phi(x_1-x_2))
$$

So rank-deficient mHC exactly computes:

$$
o_{mHC}(x)
=
x_1+x_2+\phi(\phi(x_1-x_2))
$$

Its mechanism is:

$$
Q
\rightarrow
\text{erase raw }Q
\rightarrow
\text{store }\phi(Q)
\rightarrow
\phi(\phi(Q))
$$

## General Form of Pure iHC

The same two-layer pure iHC is written as:

$$
Y_1
=
x
+
u G_1(a^Tx)
$$

$$
Y_2
=
Y_1
+
vG_2(b^TY_1)
$$

where $G_1$ and $G_2$ are arbitrary finite-width no-bias scalar SwiGLU blocks.

The output head allows an arbitrary linear head:

$$
o_{iHC}(x)
=
h^TY_2
$$

Because no-bias SwiGLU has no first-order term near the origin:

$$
G(z)=O(z^2)
$$

The first-order term of the target function is $x_1+x_2=e^Tx$, so the linear part of the output head is forced to satisfy $h=e$.

Thus, the nonlinear part of iHC must be of the form:

$$
N_{iHC}(x)
=
\alpha G_1(A)
+
\beta G_2(B+\gamma G_1(A))
$$

where $A=a^Tx$, $B=b^Tx$, $\alpha=e^Tu$, $\beta=e^Tv$, $\gamma=b^Tu$.

We require:

$$
N_{iHC}(x)
=
\phi(\phi(q^Tx))
$$

to hold for all $x$.

## Along the Zero-Mean Direction

Take:

$$
x=
\frac Q2q
=
(Q/2,-Q/2)^T
$$

Then $q^Tx=Q$ and $e^Tx=0$.

Along this line, $A$ and $B$ are both linear functions of $Q$:

$$
A=cQ
$$

$$
B=dQ
$$

Therefore, we must have:

$$
\alpha G_1(cQ)
+
\beta G_2(dQ+\gamma G_1(cQ))
=
\phi(\phi(Q))
$$

for all $Q$.

Now consider $Q\rightarrow+\infty$.

The SwiGLU primitive satisfies:

$$
\phi(Q)
=
Q^2\sigma(Q)
\sim Q^2
$$

Therefore:

$$
\phi(\phi(Q))\sim Q^4
$$

More importantly, its positive tail has no $Q^3$ polynomial term.

For iHC to produce $Q^4$, the first layer must produce a quadratic tail:

$$
G_1(cQ)
\sim
\kappa Q^2
$$

where $\kappa\ne 0$.

What the second layer reads is:

$$
dQ+\gamma G_1(cQ)
\sim
dQ+\gamma\kappa Q^2
$$

The positive tail of the second SwiGLU block is also quadratic, so:

$$
G_2(dQ+\gamma G_1(cQ))
\sim
\lambda(dQ+\gamma\kappa Q^2)^2
$$

Expanding:

$$
\lambda(dQ+\gamma\kappa Q^2)^2
=
\lambda\gamma^2\kappa^2Q^4
+
2\lambda d\gamma\kappa Q^3
+
\lambda d^2Q^2
$$

To match the target's $Q^4$, we must have $\beta\lambda\gamma\kappa\ne 0$.

But the target has no $Q^3$ term, so we must have $d=0$.

That is, the second layer read cannot read the raw $Q$ direction.

In two dimensions, $d=0$ is equivalent to $b^Tq=0$, so $b$ is parallel to $e$.

On the other hand, the target also has no $Q^2$ tail, so the part of the first layer directly visible to the output must vanish:

$$
\alpha=e^Tu=0
$$

In two dimensions, this means $u$ is parallel to $q$.

Thus:

$$
\gamma=b^Tu=0
$$

But earlier, to produce $Q^4$, we also needed $\gamma\ne 0$.

Contradiction.

Therefore, this rank-deficient mHC function cannot be exactly parameterized by a pure iHC of the same depth.


# 5. Intuition Behind the Counterexample

What mHC does is:

$$
\text{old }Q
\rightarrow
0
\rightarrow
\phi(Q)
$$

So the second layer reads a clean $\phi(Q)$.

Pure iHC cannot erase.

It can only preserve:

$$
Q+\text{new feature}
$$

So what the second layer generally reads is:

$$
dQ+\gamma\phi(Q)
$$

After another SwiGLU, a polluting term is generated:

$$
(dQ+\gamma Q^2)^2
=
\gamma^2Q^4
+
2d\gamma Q^3
+
d^2Q^2
$$

This $Q^3$ is the cost of the raw residual stream not being reset.

So what mHC actually adds is not full-rank mixing, but finite reset and reuse.


# 6. Deriving Gated iHC: iHC + Per-Direction Reset

Since what pure iHC lacks is reset, a direct correction is gated iHC:

$$
Y_{l+1}
=
D_lY_l
+
\tilde r_lF_l(\tilde p_lY_l)
$$

where $D_l$ is a diagonal gate:

$$
D_l
=
\text{diag}(g_{l,1},g_{l,2},...,g_{l,n})
$$

Intuitively, $g_{l,i}=1$ means this stream direction continues to live; $g_{l,i}=0$ means this stream direction is reset and can be reused.

In the analysis of expressive power, the specific continuous value of the gate is not the core; the zero pattern is:

$$
g_{l,i}=0
\quad\text{or}\quad
g_{l,i}\ne0
$$

That is, survive / die.

This is the core of gated iHC:

$$
\text{iHC}
+
\text{per-direction reset}
$$

## Theorem 2: Diagonal-Gated iHC Can Represent Arbitrary Static mHC

Given any static mHC:

$$
X_{l+1}
=
A_lX_l
+
r_lF_l(p_lX_l)
$$

where each $A_l$ is an arbitrary linear residual mixer. In particular, it can be the doubly stochastic matrix of mHC.

Then there exists a diagonal-gated iHC of the same depth:

$$
Y_{l+1}
=
D_lY_l
+
\tilde r_lF_l(\tilde p_lY_l)
$$

as well as a per-layer stream basis transformation $P_l$, such that $X_l=P_lY_l$, and the end-to-end outputs are equivalent.

### Key Linear Algebra Lemma

For any finite linear chain:

$$
V_0
\rightarrow
V_1
\rightarrow
V_2
\rightarrow
\cdots
\rightarrow
V_L
$$

where the linear maps between adjacent spaces are $A_0,A_1,...,A_{L-1}$. Then, one can choose a basis on each $V_l$ such that every $A_l$ is a diagonal 0/1 gate in these bases.

That is, there exist invertible matrices $P_0,P_1,...,P_L$, and diagonal matrices $E_0,E_1,...,E_{L-1}$, such that:

$$
A_lP_l=P_{l+1}E_l
$$

where:

$$
E_l=\text{diag}(0,1,1,0,...)
$$

This is essentially the interval decomposition / barcode normal form of a finite linear chain.

Each basis vector corresponds to an interval: it is born at some layer, passes through several layers, and then dies at some layer.

In this basis, each residual map does one of two things to a basis direction: either send it to the same interval's basis direction in the next layer, or kill it.

This is precisely a diagonal gate.

### Using the Lemma to Prove Simulation

By the lemma, there exist:

$$
A_lP_l=P_{l+1}E_l
$$

Let $X_l=P_lY_l$.

Construct the read/write of gated iHC:

$$
\tilde p_l=p_lP_l
$$

$$
\tilde r_l=P_{l+1}^{-1}r_l
$$

Then the block inputs are consistent:

$$
\tilde p_lY_l
=
p_lP_lY_l
=
p_lX_l
$$

So:

$$
F_l(\tilde p_lY_l)
=
F_l(p_lX_l)
$$

After the gated iHC update, multiply back by $P_{l+1}$:

$$
P_{l+1}Y_{l+1}
=
P_{l+1}E_lY_l
+
P_{l+1}\tilde r_lF_l(\tilde p_lY_l)
$$

Using $A_lP_l=P_{l+1}E_l$ and $\tilde r_l=P_{l+1}^{-1}r_l$, we get:

$$
P_{l+1}Y_{l+1}
=
A_lP_lY_l
+
r_lF_l(p_lX_l)
$$

That is:

$$
P_{l+1}Y_{l+1}
=
A_lX_l
+
r_lF_l(p_lX_l)
=
X_{l+1}
$$

So by induction, $X_l=P_lY_l$ holds for every layer.

If the final output of mHC is $o=CX_L$, then gated iHC can use $\tilde C=CP_L$:

$$
\tilde CY_L
=
CP_LY_L
=
CX_L
$$

QED.

## What Does This Theorem Actually Say?

The mHC residual matrix $A_l$ looks very general: it is an $n\times n$ matrix.

But from the perspective of the cross-layer linear chain, its essence consists of only two parts.

First, the full-rank part: change of basis.

Second, the rank-deficient part: kill directions.

The first part is already absorbable by iHC.

The second part requires a gate.

So mHC can be understood as:

$$
\text{mHC}
=
\text{iHC}
+
\text{per-direction reset}
$$

This is the significance of gated iHC.

It does not copy the Sinkhorn machinery of mHC as-is.

It directly preserves what is truly expressively meaningful in mHC: which stream directions should survive, and which stream directions should be reset and reused.


# 7. Training Problem of Gated iHC: Gates Can Cause Gradient Vanishing

The gated iHC above solves the expressivity problem, but not yet the training stability problem.

If used directly:

$$
Y_{l+1}
=
g_l\odot Y_l
+
\text{write}
$$

then diagonal gates will keep appearing in the Jacobian of the residual path.

Across many layers, roughly:

$$
D_{L-1}D_{L-2}\cdots D_0
$$

will appear.

If many $g_{l,i}&lt;1$, the gradient of the surviving path may also keep shrinking.

This is the same problem as in ordinary RNNs where repeatedly multiplying by a transition matrix leads to gradient vanishing. The reason mHC emphasizes Birkhoff / norm-preserving / identity-like residual paths is essentially to avoid the residual path going haywire in deep layers [3].

So gated iHC also needs a scale-stability design.

There are two natural directions:

1. Mean-preserving gate

2. Norm-preserving gate

Let's look at each.


# 8. Mean-Preserving Gate: Aligns with mHC, but Not the Best Answer

If one wants to mimic the doubly stochastic structure of mHC, a natural design is the mean-preserving gate.

Let:

$$
J=\frac1n\mathbf 1\mathbf 1^T
$$

It projects all streams onto the mean direction.

A simplest mean-preserving gate is:

$$
A(g)=J+g(I-J)
$$

Its meaning is:

$$
Y=\text{mean part}+\text{difference part}
$$

The mean part does not move, and the difference part is multiplied by $g$.

This design has two advantages.

First, it preserves the stream mean:

$$
\mathbf 1^TA(g)=\mathbf 1^T
$$

Second, when $0\le g\le 1$, it does not amplify zero-sum difference modes.

So it is very similar to the Birkhoff intuition of mHC: preserve the mean direction, shrink the difference direction.

But this design is not suitable as the final answer.

Reason 1: Scalar mean-preserving gate is too weak.

It can only uniformly scale all zero-sum directions. For example, when $n=3$, it cannot erase one zero-sum direction while preserving another zero-sum direction.

But mHC can achieve this partial reset.

Reason 2: Vector mean-preserving gate reintroduces mixing.

If one wants different difference directions to have different gates, while strictly preserving the mean, one usually needs to first decompose the hidden state into some zero-mean basis, then gate, then project back to the original stream basis.

This is no longer a simple per-stream diagonal gate, but reintroduces basis transform / projection / mixing.

This goes in the opposite direction of the clean iHC we want.

Reason 3: Mean preservation is not gradient scale preservation.

$A(g)=J+g(I-J)$ does preserve the mean direction, but all difference directions are still attenuated by repeated multiplication of $g$. That is, it only keeps one mean anchor from dying, and does not solve the gradient vanishing for all useful stream directions.

So the mean-preserving gate is more like mimicking the constraints of mHC, rather than directly solving the training problem of gated iHC.

In short:

**Mean-preserving gate aligns with mHC, but is too restricted and not the most direct scale-stability target.**


# 9. Norm-Preserving Gate: More Suitable for Gated iHC

If what we care about is training stability, then a more direct goal is not to preserve the mean, but to preserve the scale of the residual path.

But there is a pitfall here.

If a linear gate $R$ is required to strictly preserve the norm for all hidden states:

$$
\|RY\|=\|Y\|
$$

then it must be full-rank.

In particular, if $R$ is a diagonal matrix:

$$
R=\text{diag}(d_1,d_2,...,d_n)
$$

then:

$$
\|RY\|^2
=
\sum_i d_i^2\|Y_i\|^2
$$

To equal $\sum_i \|Y_i\|^2$ for all $Y$, we must have:

$$
|d_i|=1
$$

This means it cannot have $d_i=0$, and thus cannot reset.

So strict state-norm-preservation and reset are in conflict.

What we really want is a weaker but useful form of norm preservation:

**Preserve the RMS scale of the gate operator, while still allowing zero entries.**

That is:

$$
D(g)
=
c(g)\,\text{diag}(g)
$$

where:

$$
c(g)=\frac{\sqrt n}{\|g\|_2}
$$

This ensures:

$$
\frac1n\|D(g)\|_F^2=1
$$

If $g$ is a binary mask and there are $k$ alive streams, then:

$$
D(g)
=
\sqrt{\frac nk}\,\text{diag}(g)
$$

For example, with $n=4$ and only two streams kept, the surviving streams are multiplied by $\sqrt 2$.

This is not strictly norm-preserving for each specific hidden state, but preserves the average RMS scale of the residual gate.

It allows reset, while avoiding a systematic drop in the overall scale of the residual path when the number of gates decreases.

This is norm-gated iHC:

$$
Y_{l+1}
=
D(g_l)Y_l
+
H^{post}_lF_l(H^{pre}_lY_l)
$$

where:

$$
D(g_l)=c(g_l)\,\text{diag}(g_l)
$$

$$
c(g_l)=\frac{\sqrt n}{\|g_l\|_2}
$$

In practice, for numerical stability, one can write:

$$
c(g_l)=\frac{\sqrt n}{\|g_l\|_2+\epsilon}
$$

One can also add a cap:

$$
c(g_l)=\min\left(c_{max},\frac{\sqrt n}{\|g_l\|_2+\epsilon}\right)
$$

This avoids over-amplifying surviving streams when too few streams are alive.


# 10. Theorem 3: Norm-Gated iHC and Ordinary Gated iHC Are Equally Expressive

Now prove: norm normalization does not change expressivity.

Ordinary diagonal-gated iHC uses a binary mask:

$$
E_l=\text{diag}(m_l)
$$

where $m_l\in\{0,1\}^n$.

Norm-gated iHC uses:

$$
D_l=c_lE_l
$$

where $c_l$ is a nonzero scalar. For example:

$$
c_l=\sqrt{\frac n{k_l}}
$$

Here $k_l$ is the number of alive streams.

The key point is:

**Nonzero scalars can be absorbed by cross-layer basis scaling.**

Assume there is an ordinary gated chain:

$$
Z_{l+1}
=
E_lZ_l+\text{write}
$$

Now simulate it with a norm-gated chain:

$$
Y_{l+1}
=
D_lY_l+\text{write}
$$

Let:

$$
Z_l=s_lY_l
$$

We want the residual paths to match:

$$
s_{l+1}D_lY_l=E_lZ_l
$$

Substituting $Z_l=s_lY_l$ and $D_l=c_lE_l$, we get:

$$
s_{l+1}c_lE_lY_l=s_lE_lY_l
$$

As long as:

$$
s_{l+1}=\frac{s_l}{c_l}
$$

it holds.

So the norm-normalization scalar at each layer can be absorbed by the inter-layer scaling of the hidden basis.

Therefore:

$$
\text{ordinary diagonal-gated iHC}
=
\text{norm-gated iHC}
$$

Equally expressive.

Now combine with Theorem 2: ordinary diagonal-gated iHC can represent arbitrary static mHC.

Thus:

$$
\text{static mHC}
\subseteq
\text{norm-gated iHC}
$$

More directly, one can also rewrite the normal form of Theorem 2 into a norm-gated version.

By the normal form, there exist $P_l$ and $E_l$ such that:

$$
A_lP_l=P_{l+1}E_l
$$

Let:

$$
D_l=c_lE_l
$$

and define a new basis:

$$
Q_l=s_lP_l
$$

where:

$$
s_0=1
$$

$$
s_{l+1}=\frac{s_l}{c_l}
$$

Then:

$$
A_lQ_l=Q_{l+1}D_l
$$

This transforms the ordinary diagonal gate normal form into a norm-gated normal form.

Then let:

$$
X_l=Q_lY_l
$$

and construct:

$$
\tilde p_l=p_lQ_l
$$

$$
\tilde r_l=Q_{l+1}^{-1}r_l
$$

One obtains a simulation proof identical to Theorem 2.

So norm-gated iHC preserves the expressivity of gated iHC, while being more suitable for training stability.


# 11. Mean vs Norm: Why Norm-Gated iHC in the End?

Now we can look at the three versions together.

Pure iHC:

$$
Y_{l+1}
=
Y_l+\text{write}
$$

The advantage is that it is the cleanest, and the identity path is completely stable.

The disadvantage is that there is no reset, and it cannot exactly implement the reset-and-reuse of rank-deficient mHC.

Ordinary gated iHC:

$$
Y_{l+1}
=
g_l\odot Y_l+\text{write}
$$

The advantage is that it has reset, and expressively is sufficient to cover static mHC.

The disadvantage is that if many gates are less than 1, the scale and gradient of the residual path may decay.

Mean-preserving gated iHC:

$$
Y_{l+1}
=
\left(J+g_l(I-J)\right)Y_l+\text{write}
$$

The advantage is that it is close to the doubly-stochastic / mean-preserving intuition of mHC.

The disadvantage is that the scalar version is too weak; the vector version reintroduces projection / mixing; and it preserves the mean, not the scale of all useful directions.

Norm-gated iHC:

$$
Y_{l+1}
=
D(g_l)Y_l+\text{write}
$$

where:

$$
D(g_l)=c(g_l)\,\text{diag}(g_l)
$$

$$
c(g_l)=\frac{\sqrt n}{\|g_l\|_2+\epsilon}
$$

The advantage is that it preserves reset, while maintaining the RMS scale of the residual gate.

More importantly, it is expressively equivalent to ordinary gated iHC, because the normalization scalar can be absorbed by basis scaling.

So the final recommendation is:

**First use gated iHC to recover reset-and-reuse, then use norm normalization to stabilize the residual path scale.**

In one sentence:

**Mean-preserving is to mimic mHC; norm-gating is to make gated iHC itself train stably.**

So I lean more towards norm-gated iHC.


# 12. Caveat: Dynamic mHC

The theorems above are strict versions for static residual maps.

In real mHC, $A_l$ can be dynamically generated from the hidden state; in the mHC paper, $H^{pre}$, $H^{post}$, and $H^{res}$ can all come from dynamic projections, with $H^{res}$ then going through Sinkhorn-Knopp to obtain the constrained residual map [3].

In the dynamic case, the basis $P_l$ also becomes input-dependent:

$$
P_l=P_l(x)
$$

Thus, the exact gauge proof is no longer a fixed linear reparameterization.

But this actually strengthens the design direction of norm-gated iHC:

Do not let the controller generate a full $n\times n$ matrix, then Sinkhorn.

Let the controller directly generate a per-stream gate:

$$
g_l(x)\in[0,1]^n
$$

Plus dynamic read/write: $\tilde p_l(x)$ and $\tilde r_l(x)$.

Then use norm normalization to maintain the RMS scale of the residual gate.

This is simpler than dynamic Birkhoff projection.


# 13. Practical Suggestion: How to Implement Norm-Gated iHC?

I would start from this version:

$$
Y_{l+1}
=
D(g_l)Y_l
+
H^{post}_lF_l(H^{pre}_lY_l)
$$

where:

$$
D(g_l)=c(g_l)\,\text{diag}(g_l)
$$

$$
c(g_l)=\frac{\sqrt n}{\|g_l\|_2+\epsilon}
$$

If one is worried about surviving streams being over-amplified, a capped version can be used:

$$
c(g_l)
=
\min\left(c_{max},\frac{\sqrt n}{\|g_l\|_2+\epsilon}\right)
$$

This avoids over-amplifying surviving streams when too few streams are alive.

At initialization, let:

$$
g_l\approx \mathbf 1
$$

So the model starts close to pure iHC.

During training, if the model finds that a certain stream direction needs to be reset, it lowers the corresponding gate.

To exactly represent rank collapse, the gate should ideally be able to actually reach 0. Ordinary sigmoid can only give $g_{l,i}\in(0,1)$, so it can only approximate reset, not exactly reset.

One can consider hard-sigmoid, clipped gate, straight-through binary gate, or $\ell_0$-style gate.

If one is worried about all streams being killed, one can preserve an always-on anchor direction:

$$
g_{l,0}=1
$$

Then only gate the other streams:

$$
g_l=(1,g_{l,2},...,g_{l,n})
$$

This anchor direction does not necessarily have to bear the main computation. It is more like a stable residual backbone.


# 14. Why This Conclusion Is Natural: An Analogy from RNN to LSTM

This is actually very similar to the progression from RNN to LSTM.

The problem with ordinary RNNs is not that they "cannot express arbitrary functions," but that the state update is too uncontrolled:

$$
h_t
=
f(W_hh_{t-1}+W_xx_t)
$$

Information retention, information overwriting, and gradient propagation are all mixed together in a single nonlinear update.

The core of LSTM is not a more complex nonlinearity.

The core is splitting the state update into forget and write.

A typical form is:

$$
c_t
=
f_t\odot c_{t-1}
+
i_t\odot\tilde c_t
$$

Hochreiter and Schmidhuber's original LSTM paper addressed long-term dependency and gradient decay problems; the later forget gate also became one of the most critical reset mechanisms in modern LSTM [7].

The same applies to the residual stream.

Pure iHC is:

$$
Y_{l+1}
=
Y_l
+
\text{write}
$$

It only has write, not forget.

mHC is:

$$
X_{l+1}
=
A_lX_l
+
\text{write}
$$

It has forget / mix / reset, but uses a full matrix.

Norm-gated iHC is:

$$
Y_{l+1}
=
D(g_l)Y_l
+
\text{write}
$$

It has forget, and also has scale normalization.

This is the residual-stream version of the LSTM cell.


# 15. A Look at Horizontal Architectures: Gated Linear Attention / Gated DeltaNet

The same story also occurs in linear attention and state-space-like architectures.

Linear attention can be seen as a horizontal RNN:

$$
S_t
=
S_{t-1}
+
v_tk_t^T
$$

Readout:

$$
o_t
=
S_tq_t
$$

This is very similar to pure iHC: the state always carries identity forward, then continuously has additive writes.

The problem is also similar: if the state can only add, not forget, it becomes contaminated.

The direction of Gated Linear Attention is to add data-dependent gates, giving linear attention stronger adaptive memory control, while maintaining the parallel training / linear inference advantages of linear attention [8].

Gated DeltaNet also explicitly separates gating and delta update: gating is responsible for adaptive memory erasure, and the delta rule is responsible for targeted update [9].

This is completely parallel to our conclusion here:

$$
\text{pure additive state}
\Rightarrow
\text{clean but cannot reset}
$$

$$
\text{full matrix transition}
\Rightarrow
\text{expressive but complex}
$$

$$
\text{diagonal gate + write + scale normalization}
\Rightarrow
\text{clean and enough}
$$

So norm-gated iHC is not an isolated trick.

It is a pattern that repeatedly appears in many horizontal architectures:

$$
\text{identity memory path}
+
\text{learned forget gate}
+
\text{scale control}
+
\text{targeted write}
$$


# 16. Final Summary

mHC has value.

It tells us: multiple residual streams are useful, dynamic read/write is useful, and stream-level routing is useful.

But from the perspective of expressive power, full-rank residual mixing is just a gauge:

$$
\text{full-rank mHC}
=
\text{iHC under stream-basis change}
$$

What truly cannot be absorbed by pure iHC is rank collapse:

$$
\text{erase old direction and reuse it}
$$

And this does not require a full $n\times n$ Sinkhorn matrix.

A per-stream diagonal gate is enough:

$$
Y_{l+1}
=
g_l\odot Y_l
+
H^{post}_lF_l(H^{pre}_lY_l)
$$

However, direct gating may bring scale decay and gradient vanishing on the residual path. Therefore, a more suitable engineering version is norm-gated iHC:

$$
Y_{l+1}
=
D(g_l)Y_l
+
H^{post}_lF_l(H^{pre}_lY_l)
$$

where:

$$
D(g_l)=c(g_l)\,\text{diag}(g_l)
$$

$$
c(g_l)=\frac{\sqrt n}{\|g_l\|_2+\epsilon}
$$

To summarize in one sentence:

**The essence of mHC is not mixing, but forgetting; gated iHC preserves forgetting, and norm-gated iHC further stabilizes the scale.**

So the conclusion is:

**Identity HC is a very strong baseline; norm-gated iHC is the more natural next step.**
