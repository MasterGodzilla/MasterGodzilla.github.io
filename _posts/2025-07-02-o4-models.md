Over the past couple of days I was at UT Austin visiting a friend, and I sat in on a few physics classes with him.

One of them was on numerically solving general relativity. The instructor was an extremely energetic woman.

For the entire class, she kept telling us how complicated Einstein's equations are.

Analytical solutions? Except for a tiny handful of special cases, do not even think about it.

For numerical solutions, you first have to prove the whole PDE is well posed. I do not really know what that means; it seems to be something like, if you perturb a solution a little, it is still a solution, or something along those lines. Then you have to find an initial condition before you can even start computing.

That does not sound too complicated, but for a very concrete case, it took humanity until 1950 to get this far for the first time.

And for more complicated cases, today's methods are basically one custom method per situation. There is almost no generality to speak of.

As a machine learning student doing pseudo-science plus pseudo-math plus pseudo-engineering, I only half understood the lecture. After class I wanted to ask a question, but I could not come up with anything genuinely physics-related, so I had to ask:

"If AI breaks through in the next couple of years, say o4-o5-level models appear, and they can reason in mathematical physics at the level of an ordinary mathematician, then if I gave you ten billion mediocre mathematicians like that, what previously unsolved problems would become solvable?"

I originally thought she would say something like: AI could classify all kinds of edge cases in much more detail, then gradually build up a macro-level picture. Similar to how, when the four-color theorem was solved, computers divided all possible topological cases into billions of categories and checked them one by one.

But her answer was more interesting:

"Right now, the numerical methods used to solve Einstein's equations are very slow and not very accurate. Most of them use first-order finite difference methods to estimate the derivatives in the partial differential equations.

"The main reason is that physicists are already overwhelmed by all the mathematical prerequisites.

"For first-order differences alone, there are ten formulas. The first four are used to determine a snapshot solution in time, meaning the spatial state at a particular instant. The remaining six are used to evolve it forward in time.

"If you want to do a higher-order algorithm like Runge-Kutta, it is possible that deriving the expansion alone would take mathematicians hundreds of lifetimes. Humans cannot afford to derive it. So no such algorithm exists right now.

"With AI, though, expanding numerical methods for PDE should be pure labor and should be automatable. A higher-order algorithm designed this way might have tens of thousands of lines of formulas and hundreds of thousands of terms, but its accuracy and convergence speed would both be much better."

That answer really shocked me.

We know that improvements in intelligence mainly come from scaling: more data and more compute. Pretraining, post-training, and the newly popular test-time compute all work this way.

But this was the first time I realized that scaling can also be applied to the order of a Taylor expansion.

It turns out that people use low-order approximations mostly because they are being lazy, or rather because the math is too much work and too exhausting to calculate.

In the future, with AI, it will not matter how many lines of formulas there are.

At that point, even if there is only a tiny gain in precision, as long as nothing forbids it, expand it to twelfth order and see what happens. Let it fail before we say the theory has a problem.

Thinking one step further: before AI, intelligence was expensive. In the future, it will be cheap. Without AI, if figuring something out clearly did not create at least tens of thousands of dollars of value, nobody was going to hire a PhD to study it properly. Later on, even if something only has a few cents of value, we should still ask AI to calculate it clearly, because the electricity costs only a few cents.

So everyone should broaden their imagination. Beyond the order of Taylor expansion, there must be many other pure-labor things in the future that automation can push to the extreme. It is very interesting to think about.

Feel the AGI!

To clarify what I am trying to discuss here: if we can stack up a huge amount of simple intelligence, what kind of qualitative change, or emergent capability, can that quantitative change produce?

As an analogy, imagine we are Euler or Gauss, sitting there by hand calculating all kinds of ODEs.

In that traditional domain, modern people are not necessarily stronger than those two.

Now suppose we obtain a computer that can perform an enormous number of simple calculations.

The trivial, obvious idea is that we can do all kinds of arithmetic with large and small numbers. We no longer need to copy out a table of logarithms from 1 to 100000 by hand. Analytical solutions can also be calculated more accurately.

But the non-trivial, non-obvious idea is that for ODEs where no analytical solution can be found, we can directly brute-force a numerical solution.

For people in the nineteenth century, because the cost was too high, that idea would have been rejected by consciousness the moment it appeared in the subconscious. It would not even have had a chance to enter serious discussion.

They were even less likely to imagine that this technology could become cheap enough to use in a video game's physics engine. Even more so, if you know what I mean.

This is one kind of qualitative change produced by quantitative change.

So let us imagine: if future AI can perform an enormous amount of simple mathematical reasoning, what can it do that could not be done before?

The obvious idea is that it can automate the mental labor humans already do today.

But the non-obvious ideas are more interesting to discuss: operations we subconsciously reject because they are too expensive.

This post is trying to propose one such dimension: the complexity of numerical methods.

Likewise, in the future there should be many more ways of using ultra-cheap intelligence that are still beyond our current imagination.
