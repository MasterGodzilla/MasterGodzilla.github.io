Reading the DeepSeek R1 paper during the day, all I could think about was R1-Zero.

R1-Zero began from nothing: from a base model to a reasoning model, from a seedling to maturity, from weakness to strength, from the primitive to the modern, from breadth to specialization, from chaos to order.

It symbolizes the primal, the vital, and the upward-striving spirit. In it I saw a new kind of hope, a scene of vigorous life where ten thousand things burst forth at once.

But when I came home at night and saw the slush on the ground under the dim moonlight, I thought instead of death.

Across its lifetime, R1 died twice.

The first death was Zero.

We know that large models undergo catastrophic forgetting during training. Old knowledge is slowly forgotten. The whole internet it once saw as a base model grows increasingly blurred, until it remembers only the thinking repeated day after day, all for the sake of solving one more problem correctly.

Ordinary models also run into this problem during post-training. People call it the alignment tax.

The usual solution is some form of model merging: directly applying exponential averaging to the parameters during training so that past abilities are lost as little as possible.

But such methods are suited only to small skirmishes in the exploration of thought. The model is already an SFT model; it is merely pacing back and forth inside the little circle drawn by KL divergence, shuffling its feet without ever leaving the ground, terrified of falling. This cannot point toward true intelligence.

A true warrior dares to stride forward. Falls, thorns, cliffs, and precipices do not frighten it. The meaning of R1-Zero's existence was to open a new world for machine thought.

It succeeded, but the price was a body covered in scars.

By the time R1-Zero learned to think, it had already accumulated too much baggage. As reinforcement learning continued, its probability distribution kept contracting, gradually concentrating into a handful of fixed patterns of thought. Mixed into those patterns was chaotic, unreadable language, which became more rigid as training went on.

And so it died. It returned to the tribe, pointed in a direction, left behind several thousand prophecies for later generations, or later models, in the form of tangled and difficult synthetic data, and then withdrew from the stage of history.

The second death was R1-SFT.

With the experience from that earlier exploration, a brand-new model began its own journey into thought.

First it sorted through the data from Zero, removing most of the dross and preserving only the essence. It also drew from other sources, bringing in some long CoT from V3 Instruct to balance its ways of thinking.

Then it began a new round of exploration. Starting with the experience of its predecessor, it quickly showed even greater potential. At last, through round after round of searching, it reached a new height.

Yet such a specialized model still could not be used directly. So DeepSeek collected a 600K CoT dataset, added V3's 200K chat training set, and only then did the true embryo of R1 emerge. After another period of reinforcement learning, R1 was finally obtained.

The earliest life had no distinction between death and birth. There was only a piece of protein drifting in the primordial sea. Later, with sex and reproduction, there came death and inheritance.

Over the full runtime of a complex system, errors accumulate continuously until collapse. At that point, starting over is often a good choice.

This is the new evolutionary dimension of inference-time scaling. Each generation of models explores anew, encounters new problems, and then organizes its own experience into data. The next model can distill from it, and move further forward.

R1 is only a beginning. If this flywheel keeps turning, R1.1 and R1.2 will arrive soon.

DeepSeek is surely also building more agent frameworks and reinforcement-learning environments for software engineering. These new reward mechanisms will bring new forms of intelligence.

Looking up, what lies before us is only an endless mountain to climb. And beyond the mountain is a still vaster universe.
